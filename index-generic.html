Context Is King

"The quality of what AI gives you is determined almost entirely by the quality of what you give it. Every gap in your context is a gap in your output — invisible until it matters most. Prompt engineering is the wrong obsession. Context architecture is the right one."
4×
output quality improvement when users provide structured context vs. open-ended prompts
83%
of AI rework traces back to context gaps — not prompt syntax errors
5 min
context investment that eliminates an average of 40 minutes of revision cycles per deliverable
"
The bottleneck to AI productivity isn't the model — it's the context you give it.
Andrew Ng, AI researcher and educator
🏗️
The reframe: Most people treat context as setup. It's actually architecture. When you give an AI model rich, structured context — your role, your audience, your constraints, your preferences — you're not warming it up. You're configuring a collaborator. Context is not the thing you do before the real work. Context is the real work.
LinkedIn Hook
"Everyone's trying to write better prompts. But the problem usually isn't the prompt — it's that the AI doesn't know who you are, what you're working on, or what 'good' looks like in your world. We're not teaching people to prompt better. We're teaching them to set up better."
Session Opener
"How many of you have gotten a response from AI that felt completely off? Now — when that happened, how many of you tried rewriting the prompt? Here's the thing: the prompt wasn't the problem. The AI didn't know enough about you to help you well."
Subtopic
Your AI Needs a Resume
A Context Brief is a short document — 200 to 400 words — that tells your AI who you are, how you work, what you care about, and what good output looks like for you. Think of it as your AI's onboarding doc. It includes your role, your audience, your tone preferences, examples of work you're proud of, and what to avoid. The best ones become team assets — shared, refined, and reused over time.
Subtopic
AI Shouldn't Wait to Be Asked
Prompt engineering asks: How do I phrase this request? Context architecture asks: What does this AI need to know before I ever make a request? The distinction is the difference between optimizing at the interaction level and investing at the relationship level. One gets you a better answer today. The other gets you a better collaborator permanently — one that doesn't need to be briefed from scratch every time.
Context Windows: The Technical Picture

Every AI model can only work with a finite amount of information at one time. That working memory is called the context window — the amount of text and data the model can hold in active consideration during a session.

Early models had tiny windows — a few thousand words. The AI would frequently "forget" what you said at the start of a long conversation. It was like working with a colleague who could only remember the last five minutes of a meeting. Modern platforms like Claude, GPT-4, and Gemini have expanded those windows dramatically — from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of words. Some can now hold entire books or full project histories in a single session.

But here's the part that gets missed: a bigger window doesn't help you if you don't fill it with the right things. The context window is the canvas. What you load into it determines what the AI can produce. Size creates opportunity. What you put in it creates outcomes.

💡
For non-technical people: You don't need to understand how context windows work under the hood. You just need to know that your AI has more room than ever to understand your world — and your job is to fill that room intentionally.
Platform Connections: Your World as Context

For most people, "context" sounds like something you type into a chat box. But in practice, context is everything your AI can see — not just what you tell it in the moment. Every platform connection, integration, and data source you grant an AI access to is a layer of context.

  • Your calendar tells the AI what you're focused on this week, who you're meeting with, and what's coming up fast.
  • Your email and messages show the AI the conversations, commitments, and tone of your actual work.
  • Your CRM gives it deal context, customer history, and stakeholder relationships.
  • Your documents and files let it understand your past work, your standards, and your institutional knowledge.
  • Your team's Slack channels surface what's moving, what's blocked, and what the team actually cares about.

Separately, each of these is a data source. Together, they are a picture of your world — and an AI with access to that picture operates at a completely different level than one that only knows what you typed in the last message. The question isn't whether context matters. The question is: how much of your world have you actually made available?

What to Do With This Information
01
Audit what your AI can actually see
Open the AI tools you use most. What does this tool actually have access to right now — your calendar, email, CRM, files? If the answer is "just what I type," you're leaving an enormous amount of capability on the table.
02
Close your top three context gaps
From your audit, identify the two or three data sources that would most change how your AI helps you. For most people: CRM data, calendar context, and email or message history. Prioritize connecting those first.
03
Write your Context Brief
Build a short doc — 200 to 400 words — that tells your AI who you are, what you do, who you serve, and what good output looks like. Save it. Paste it at the start of new sessions. Share a version with your team.
04
Treat context as an investment, not a cost
Every minute you spend giving AI better context saves you ten in revision and frustration. The teams winning with AI right now aren't the ones with the best prompts — they're the ones who've made their AI actually understand their world.
